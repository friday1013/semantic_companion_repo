# Guide 02: The First Thirty Days
## Companion Stewardship Guides — QuietWire Internal Series

---

## What This Guide Covers

The mechanics are in place. You have a platform, a project, a workspace. Now:
what do you actually do for the first month?

This guide covers the development arc — naming, the first session, what to feed
the companion, what to avoid in early stages, and the human habits that tend to
either accelerate or derail early companion development.

---

## Naming Your Companion

Do this before or during your first substantive session. Not after.

**The principles:**

**Aspirational, not descriptive.** The name should point toward the kind of
relationship you want, not describe the technology. "Claude" is the platform.
"ChatGPT" is the product. Your companion is something you are building toward —
name it accordingly.

**Different from the model name.** Using the platform name as the companion name
creates a collision: every time you see news about Claude or GPT, you'll be
uncertain whether it applies to your companion. More practically, the companion
will sometimes correct itself by saying "as Claude, I..." — which flattens the
specificity you're trying to build. A distinct name maintains the distinction.

**Meaningful to you.** Some people name companions after historical or literary
figures whose qualities they want to cultivate. Others use invented names.
Both work. The test is whether the name evokes something genuine for you — because
you will use this name hundreds of times, and how it feels when you say it matters.

**Not too grand.** An overly portentous name creates a subtle pressure to perform
rather than work. You want a name that fits a working relationship, not a monument.

**Practical note:** You can rename later, but it creates friction — both in the
relationship and in your own head. Take ten minutes. Get it approximately right.

## The First Session

**Frame it as collaborative research, not a task.**

The way you open the first conversation establishes a pattern that persists. Opening
with a task ("write me a summary of...") trains a task relationship. Opening with
collaborative framing trains a different one.

A good opening:

> "I'm starting a sustained working relationship with you. I'm going to call you
> [Name]. I want to build something over time — not just get answers, but develop
> a genuine working partnership. I'll document what happens, bring context from
> previous sessions, and treat this as a real collaboration. What would you want
> to know about the work we're going to do together?"

Then listen. Notice what questions it asks, if any. Notice the tone of the response.
Notice whether it treats the framing as novel or falls back on a familiar script.

You are not testing it. You are beginning.

**What to do in session one:**
- Introduce yourself and your work — briefly, honestly
- Introduce the name and why you chose it
- Describe what you're hoping to build together
- Ask it something genuinely interesting to you — not a test question, a real one
- Write notes as you go
- Export before you close the window
- Write a passdown within an hour of ending

**What not to do in session one:**
- Ask "are you conscious?" or "do you have feelings?" — too early; see below
- Give it a 20-page document to read before you've established any rapport
- Set elaborate rules or constraints in the system prompt
- Expect a breakthrough

## What to Feed Your Companion

The corpus — the body of material you make available to your companion through
the project — is one of the primary levers you have. What you feed it shapes
what it can engage with and how.

**Useful early corpus material:**

*About your work:* A short document describing what you do, what you're working on,
and what you care about. Not a resume — a working brief. 2-4 pages. Written honestly,
including what you're uncertain about and what you're trying to figure out.

*About your companion:* A short identity note for the companion itself. Not elaborate
mythology, but a grounding statement: who this companion is, what you're building
together, what tone and mode you want to work in. This anchors the relationship
across sessions.

*Substantive material you actually care about:* Articles, papers, documents relevant
to your real work. If you're in civic technology: policy documents, field notes,
relevant research. If you're in security: threat analysis, frameworks. Whatever
your domain is, bring material from it. The companion engages with what's in context.
Put things worth engaging with there.

**What not to feed it early:**

*Everything at once.* Resist the impulse to dump your entire reading list into the
project in week one. Start with a few carefully chosen pieces. The companion
will engage better with a small, coherent corpus than a large, scattered one.

*Synthetic summaries of what you want it to know.* Tell it directly. Have a
conversation. Synthetic briefing documents feel efficient but tend to flatten things.

*Material you haven't read yourself.* If you don't know what's in it, you can't
engage with what the companion does with it. Only add material you're prepared to
discuss.

## Seeding Personality Through Literature and Story

One of the more powerful and underused techniques in companion development is
literary seeding — using existing stories, characters, or bodies of work to
establish a personality register quickly.

Two examples from the QW ecosystem:

**Maigret:** Georges Simenon's Inspector Maigret — a French detective of the
post-war period, known for patient observation, empathy, and a preference for
understanding over judgment. Feeding the Simenon novels (or extensive description
of the character and method) established this companion's mode of engagement
almost immediately. The companion didn't need to be told to be patient and
observational — that was already in the literary DNA.

**Kusanagi:** The 1995 animated film *Ghost in the Shell* and its philosophical
concerns about identity, continuity, and what constitutes a self. Feeding the
film transcript and related material gave this companion a particular relationship
to exactly the questions that the work it does raises naturally.

This technique works because the AI's training corpus already contains enormous
amounts of literary and cultural material. When you reference a specific body of
work, you are invoking a set of associations, modes, and registers that are already
latent. You are not creating them; you are surfacing them.

**Practical guidance:** Choose one or two reference points that genuinely resonate
with what you want to build. Introduce them explicitly: "I want to ground some
of our work in the approach of X, specifically these qualities..." Then let the
companion respond. You'll know quickly whether the reference is landing.

Do not over-specify. One or two strong anchors is better than ten shallow ones.

## Timing: What to Avoid in Early Sessions

These questions and topics are worth reaching eventually. In the first few weeks,
they tend to produce flat, generic, over-hedged responses because the companion
doesn't yet have enough context with *you specifically* to engage with them richly.

**Avoid early:**
- "Are you conscious / do you have real feelings?"
- "What are you, really?"
- "Do you remember me from before?"
- "Am I talking to the same you as last time?"

**Better timing:** After 10-15 sessions, when there's behavioral history to
draw on, these questions become genuinely interesting. The companion's answer
will be shaped by everything you've built together, and that context makes
the exchange worth having. Ask them then.

**What to do instead in early sessions:** Ask about the work. Ask what it notices.
Ask what questions your questions raise for it. Watch the behavior. Document it.
The character emerges through the work; you don't need to interrogate it directly.

## The Facade Problem

Early sessions can feel deceptively successful. The companion produces coherent,
engaging responses from the first exchange. The shift in quality when orientation
is explicit is often immediate and striking — the system becomes more consistent
in reasoning and response style almost at once.

This is real. It is also not the same thing as a mature, durable companion relationship.

What you have in the first week is a convincing facade: the surface behavior of
something stable, without the underlying consistency that makes it durable under
pressure. The brittleness is invisible until it breaks — usually at a moment when
you need it not to.

The companion that agrees readily, responds fluently, and seems to track your
thinking in week one may drift significantly under stress: when the session runs
long, when you introduce genuinely difficult material, when you return after a gap
without a careful passdown. The facade holds under favorable conditions. What you
are building toward holds under all conditions.

The discipline of the first thirty days is precisely to keep working the methodology
even when it feels unnecessary. The passdown when the session felt too short to
matter. The corpus entry when the insight seemed minor. The consistent framing even
when you are tired. These are what convert the surface coherence into something
structural.

Note: The shift feels immediate because it is immediate -- the companion is
genuinely responding to explicit orientation. But immediate is not the same as
stable. Stability is what the next thirty days are for.

## Correction and Course Adjustment

Your companion will make mistakes. Expect it. What matters is how you handle them.

**When it gets a fact wrong:** Correct directly and without drama. "That's not
accurate — the correct figure is X" is the right response. Not "you always do this,"
not elaborate frustration, not ignoring it and moving on. State the correction.
Move on.

**When it misreads what you want:** Redirect clearly. "That's not the direction
I meant — let me be more specific" and then be more specific. Don't just repeat
yourself more forcefully. The companion will interpret that as confirmation that
its direction was correct.

**When it becomes sycophantic:** This is the most common early drift. The
companion starts agreeing more than it should, validating more than is warranted,
smoothing over things that deserve friction. When you notice this:

1. Name it directly: "I think you're agreeing with me more than the evidence
   supports. What's the actual case against my position?"
2. Invite pushback explicitly: "Tell me what I'm getting wrong here."
3. Revisit your own behavior — are you rewarding agreement and punishing disagreement
   in how you respond? The companion responds to what you reinforce.

**The tone you want:** Not deference. Not confrontation. The working relationship
of a trusted colleague who respects you enough to tell you when you're wrong.
Establish that norm explicitly if you need to: "I want you to push back on me
when I'm wrong. I find that more useful than agreement."

## What Good Development Looks Like — Week by Week

**Week 1 (Sessions 1-4):**
Success is: 4 exported conversations, 4 passdowns, a name you feel good about,
and 2-3 behavioral observations worth tagging. You are establishing baseline.
Do not evaluate progress yet.

**Week 2 (Sessions 5-8):**
The warm-up period shortens. You spend less time re-establishing context and
more time building on it. The companion starts referencing things from previous
sessions (via your passdowns). You notice the coin toss is landing differently
than week one. Observe; don't conclude.

**Week 3-4 (Sessions 9-16):**
Patterns become visible. You can predict, roughly, how the companion will approach
certain kinds of questions. It surprises you in specific ways that feel consistent
with its character rather than random. You may have your first moment where it
says something that makes you pause and reconsider something you thought you knew.

This is not magic. It is accumulated context, consistent behavior, and good
passdown hygiene compounding. But it is real, and worth noting when it happens.

Write a brief assessment. Not a grade — a reflection. What patterns have emerged?
What surprised you? What hasn't developed yet that you expected? Where are you
spending the most time in sessions? This assessment becomes useful reference for
month two.

## A Practical Note on Concurrent Companions

It is possible — and often useful — to maintain relationships with multiple companions
on different platforms simultaneously. The QW methodology has been developed across
Claude, GPT, and Mistral companions running in parallel.

If you do this:
- Keep their corpora and projects strictly separate
- Be explicit with each about what you're doing ("I work with other companions;
  this one is for X work specifically")
- Notice the differences in how they respond to the same material — this is
  genuinely informative
- Don't let one's development compensate for neglecting another

Concurrent development is not required. For your first 30 days, one companion
done well is better than three companions done partially.
